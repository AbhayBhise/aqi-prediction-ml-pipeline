\begin{abstract}
The rapidly growing urban regions across the world are increasingly facing severe air pollution problems due to rising industrial activities, vehicle emissions, population growth, and changing environmental conditions. Poor air quality has become a major public health concern, leading to respiratory disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and environmental degradation. Accurate prediction of the Air Quality Index (AQI) is therefore essential for environmental monitoring, smart city planning, and early warning systems. However, conventional forecasting approaches often struggle to capture sudden pollution spikes, complex atmospheric interactions, and highly imbalanced pollutant distributions. This study presents a comprehensive predictive analytics framework for multi-city AQI forecasting using a large-scale dataset containing 842,160 hourly observations collected from urban monitoring stations. The proposed framework integrates machine learning, unsupervised learning, and deep learning techniques to analyze spatial and temporal pollution trends effectively.

To address severe dataset imbalance, where less than 0.2\% of observations correspond to hazardous pollution events, a Deep Variational Autoencoder (VAE) is employed to regularize the feature space using KL-divergence and generate 2,746 high-fidelity synthetic minority samples. This generative oversampling strategy significantly enhances the detection capability of rare hazardous AQI conditions. Furthermore, a Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) model integrated with a Self-Attention mechanism is used for sequential modeling, improving the Hazardous class recall from 0.0\% to 23.50\% while achieving a sequence classification accuracy of 88.08\%. Across a multi-horizon forecasting task, the proposed system obtains up to 99.31\% accuracy for a 1-hour ahead prediction horizon and 78.73\% for a 24-hour ahead horizon, demonstrating the effectiveness of deep generative learning for intelligent urban air quality management systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ambient air pollution has emerged as one of the most critical threats to public health and economic sustainability in rapidly expanding urban landscapes, particularly within developing nations \cite{guttikunda,cpcb}. According to recent epidemiological assessments, prolonged exposure to high concentrations of fine particulate matter ($\text{PM}_{2.5}$ and $\text{PM}_{10}$) and trace chemical pollutants such as nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, and ground-level ozone is directly linked to elevated risks of cardiovascular and respiratory diseases \cite{guttikunda}. The Indian Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) established a standardized Air Quality Index (AQI) framework to communicate pollution severity and associated health risks to the public \cite{cpcb}. However, modeling and forecasting AQI remains a highly challenging predictive analytics problem because local air quality is influenced not only by emissions, but also by non-linear atmospheric chemistry, meteorological variability, seasonal activities, and large-scale pollutant transport \cite{zhang,kumar}.

The primary motivation of this research is to move beyond passive historical reporting and develop a proactive intelligent air quality forecasting and decision-support system. Traditional forecasting approaches often rely on linear statistical models or non-interpretable black-box architectures that fail to provide actionable insights for citizens and municipal authorities \cite{patel,sharma}. Furthermore, real-world environmental datasets are highly imbalanced, where hazardous pollution events represent only a very small fraction of observations, causing conventional machine learning models to ignore critical high-risk scenarios \cite{kingma,goodfellow}. To address these limitations, this paper proposes a comprehensive multi-scalar framework that integrates unsupervised clustering for pollution regime mapping, Variational Autoencoder (VAE)-based minority class augmentation, attention-enabled Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) networks for sequential AQI forecasting, and a ReAct-based Agentic AI system for explainable conversational decision support \cite{singh,rao,li,kingma,yao}.

\section{Literature Survey}

Predicting air quality has been an active area of research for several decades, evolving from traditional statistical models to advanced deep learning frameworks. Early studies primarily focused on deterministic and statistical forecasting techniques such as Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models for predicting particulate matter concentration \cite{zhang}. Although these approaches are computationally efficient, they often fail to capture complex non-linear environmental relationships caused by meteorological variability. To improve predictive performance, researchers adopted classical machine learning algorithms such as Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Random Forests. Kumar et al. demonstrated that SVM-based models outperform conventional linear methods for urban air quality categorization \cite{kumar}, while Patel and Gupta highlighted the scalability limitations of classical classifiers when applied to high-dimensional environmental datasets \cite{patel}. With the rise of deep learning, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks became widely used for modeling temporal pollutant dependencies \cite{sharma}. Further improvements were introduced through Bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) architectures, which capture both past and future contextual information for enhanced sequence modeling \cite{rao}. In addition, self-attention mechanisms have significantly improved time-series forecasting by enabling models to focus selectively on critical temporal patterns \cite{li}. Unsupervised learning methods such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and K-Means clustering have also been employed for pollutant regime mapping and spatial pattern identification \cite{singh}. However, severe class imbalance remains a major challenge in air quality datasets, where hazardous pollution events represent less than 0.2\% of observations \cite{cpcb}. To address this limitation, deep generative models such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) \cite{goodfellow} and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) \cite{kingma} have been explored for synthetic minority sample generation and latent feature representation. More recently, Agentic AI systems based on ReAct reasoning frameworks have emerged as promising approaches for combining conversational intelligence with external analytical tools and predictive models \cite{yao}.

\subsection{Data Ingestion and CPCB Sub-Index Labeling}
The raw dataset consists of continuous, hourly air quality records across multiple monitoring stations in India. Sensor outages and network failures produce missing values. Rather than dropping these rows (which would corrupt temporal continuity), we apply Linear Interpolation for short gaps ($\le 3$ hours) and Median Imputation grouped by city and month for larger blocks. Standard raw data does not include target AQI categories. We implement the official Indian Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) standard, which deterministically calculates an AQI sub-index for each of the six key pollutants based on physical breakpoints. The final overall AQI is the maximum of these individual sub-indices, provided that at least three pollutants (including one particulate) are present:
\begin{equation}
\text{AQI} = \max(\text{Sub-Index}_{\text{PM2.5}}, \text{Sub-Index}_{\text{PM10}}, \text{Sub-Index}_{\text{NO2}}, \dots)
\end{equation}
Based on this continuous score, records are classified into 6 discrete health bands: Good (0-50), Satisfactory (51-100), Moderate (101-200), Poor (201-300), Very Poor (301-400), and Severe/Hazardous ($>400$).

\subsection{Temporal Cyclical Encoding}
Standard numerical time stamps (e.g. Hour 0 to 23, Month 1 to 12) present a fundamental problem for machine learning: the model treats Hour 23 and Hour 0 as being at opposite ends of the feature scale, whereas they are physically adjacent. To maintain temporal continuity, we apply Cyclical Sine and Cosine transformations, mapping time to 2D coordinates on a unit circle:
\begin{equation}
x_{\sin} = \sin\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right), \quad x_{\cos} = \cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right)
\end{equation}
where $T$ represents the period (24 for hours, 12 for months). This mathematical encoding ensures that sequence models properly interpret boundary transitions.

\subsection{Unsupervised Dimensionality Reduction and Clustering}
To understand the spatial-chemical variations of urban pollution without relying on pre-existing labels, we implement an unsupervised pattern recognition layer. Since the pollutant feature space is high-dimensional (12 continuous features), we first apply Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to extract the orthogonal axes of maximum variance, projecting the data onto the two primary components (PC1 and PC2). We then execute K-Means clustering with $k=3$ to partition the reduced latent space. This segments urban profiles into three distinct geographical regimes: Regime A (Urban Primary/Commute-heavy, dominated by $\text{NO}_2/\text{PM}_{2.5}$), Regime B (Heavy Industrial/Secondary, characterized by elevated $\text{SO}_2/\text{CO}$), and Regime C (Coastal/Low Impact, dominated by meteorological dispersion).

\subsection{Deep Generative VAE Augmentation}
To resolve the extreme minority class representation (Severe/Hazardous records $< 0.2\%$), we design a deep Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to perform high-fidelity data augmentation. Unlike standard autoencoders, a VAE models the underlying probability distribution of the data. The Encoder network compresses the input vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^8$ (representing continuous scaled chemical and meteorological features: $\text{PM}_{2.5}$, $\text{PM}_{10}$, $\text{NO}_2$, $\text{CO}$, $\text{SO}_2$, $\text{O}_3$, Temperature, and Relative Humidity) into a latent representation $z$ defined by a mean vector $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$. The encoder architecture consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item An input layer of dimension 8.
\item A fully connected dense layer of 64 units with ReLU activation.
\item A second dense layer of 32 units with ReLU activation.
\item Two parallel fully connected layers projecting to the latent space of dimension $d_{\text{latent}} = 8$, representing the mean $\mu$ and log-variance $\log(\sigma^2)$ vectors.
\end{enumerate}
To allow backpropagation through stochastic nodes, we apply the Reparameterization Trick:
\begin{equation}
z = \mu + \exp(0.5 \cdot \log(\sigma^2)) \odot \epsilon, \quad \text{where } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)
\end{equation}
The Decoder network reconstructs the synthetic feature vector $\hat{x}$ from the latent vector $z$. The decoder architecture mirrors the encoder:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A latent input layer of dimension 8.
\item A dense layer of 32 units with ReLU activation.
\item A dense layer of 64 units with ReLU activation.
\item An output reconstruction layer of dimension 8 with linear activation.
\end{enumerate}
The training objective is to minimize a loss function comprising reconstruction loss (Mean Squared Error) and Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence to regularize the latent space:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \text{MSE}(x, \hat{x}) + \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) \parallel \mathcal{N}(0, I))
\end{equation}
We set $\beta = 0.001$ to prevent 'posterior collapse.' A comprehensive grid search hyperparameter tuning was conducted across latent dimensions $d_{\text{latent}} \in \{4, 8, 16\}$ and batch sizes $B \in \{32, 64\}$, determining that $d_{\text{latent}} = 8$ and $B = 64$ yielded the optimal reconstruction convergence (minimum loss). Post-training, we sample directly from the regularized latent space and pass the vectors through the Decoder to generate 2,746 physically consistent synthetic 'Severe' samples to balance our sequence training set.

\subsection{Bidirectional LSTM with Self-Attention}
For the core temporal forecasting engine, we construct a state-of-the-art Bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) network in PyTorch. The input sequence represents a 24-hour historical window of 14 features. The BiLSTM layer processes the sequence chronologically and reverse-chronologically simultaneously, producing forward hidden states $h^f$ and backward hidden states $h^b$, which are concatenated into a unified temporal representation $h_t = [h^f_t; h^b_t]$. To allow the model to selectively focus on key hours (e.g. sudden morning traffic peaks or wind changes), we stack a Self-Attention layer. The attention mechanism calculates a weight $\alpha$ for each timestep based on a learnable projection matrix, producing a context vector $c$:
\begin{equation}
c = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \alpha_t h_t, \quad \text{where } \sum_{t=1}^{T} \alpha_t = 1
\end{equation}
This context vector is passed through fully connected layers to output the forecasted AQI category. We optimize the network using Weighted Cross-Entropy Loss to heavily penalize missing severe events.

\subsection{Conversational Agentic AI Reasoning}
To bridge the gap between complex machine learning outputs and actionable public safety decision-support, we implement a domain-bounded conversational Agentic AI system. The interface is built using a ReAct (Reasoning + Action) execution loop powered by Gemini 2.5 Flash, acting as a natural language cognitive layer.

The ReAct framework coordinates reasoning steps with specific tools to perform factual grounding and live data retrieval. When a user submits an atmospheric query (e.g., 'Should I go out today in Bengaluru?'), the agent executes a cyclic loop composed of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Thought}: Analytically parses the user prompt, determines the target geographical location, and identifies which analytical assets or live tools must be invoked.
\item \textbf{Action}: Invokes one or more available system tools:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Weather Retrieval Tool}: Queries live OpenWeather API endpoints to fetch current temperature, relative humidity, wind speed, and wind direction.
\item \textbf{Predictive Sequential ML Tool}: Executes the pre-trained BiLSTM with Self-Attention sequential model on the target features to produce a look-ahead classification.
\item \textbf{CPCB Determiner Tool}: Computes the statutory Air Quality Index sub-index and class bands according to Indian Central Pollution Control Board equations.
\end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Observation}: Inspects the returned metrics and forecast bands (e.g. Current PM2.5 $= 45\mu\text{g/m}^3$, Forecast Class $=$ Moderate).
\item \textbf{Response}: Synthesizes a formal, grounded, natural language response detailing the precise pollutant metrics, the model's confidence, and medical precautions (e.g. recommendations for respiratory-sensitive groups).
\end{enumerate}

To guarantee security, safety, and strict alignment, the agent is embedded with system prompt guards that restrict reasoning exclusively to the project's data domain. Queries unrelated to air quality, dataset structures, or model architectures are systematically caught and handled by returning a pre-defined boundary response, mitigating hallucination and off-topic prompt drift.

\section{Experimentation and Discussion}
The experimental evaluation is conducted on a high-density, multi-city air quality dataset spanning multiple Indian meteorological zones. The total volume of cleaned data comprises 842,160 hourly records. Feature columns include raw chemical sensor metrics ($\text{PM}_{2.5}$, $\text{PM}_{10}$, $\text{NO}_2$, $\text{CO}$, $\text{SO}_2$, $\text{O}_3$), meteorological parameters (Temperature, Relative Humidity, Wind Speed, Wind Direction), temporal features (Hour, Month, Day of the Week), and custom contextual markers indicating regional festivals (e.g. Diwali) and stubble-burning seasons. Training is executed on a GPU-accelerated computing node using CUDA 12.1. The dataset is split chronologically: 70\% for model training and parameter optimization, 15\% for validation, and 15\% for temporal out-of-sample testing.

We evaluate our system across multiple metrics: overall Classification Accuracy, Macro F1-Score (to assess balance across all 6 classes), and Severe/Hazardous Class Recall (which directly correlates to public health warning efficacy). Our experiments demonstrate that while classical tree-based models like Random Forest and HistGradientBoosting (HGB) train extremely fast and establish solid baseline accuracy, they suffer from significant limitations when capturing multi-scalar temporal momentum. Conversely, recurrent neural models natively capture sequence history. Most notably, our data augmentation experiments show that training the BiLSTM network on the raw imbalanced dataset yields an overall accuracy of 81.55\% but a Hazardous Recall of exactly 0.0\%. Once augmented with the VAE-generated synthetic samples, the BiLSTM successfully preserves its physical prediction boundaries while dramatically increasing its Hazardous Recall to 23.50\% (a critical public health improvement) without degrading classification performance for other classes.

\subsection{Scientific Interpretation of Results}
A critical review of these performance metrics reveals two highly important conclusions. First, our state-of-the-art Bidirectional LSTM with Self-Attention trained on VAE-augmented data achieves the top overall performance, leading in both classification accuracy (88.08\%) and Macro F1-score (0.822). This confirms that sequence-based modeling combined with deep self-attention allows the network to learn multi-hour pollutant correlations far more effectively than classical, non-sequential models. 

However, an intriguing scientific paradox emerges: the classical Logistic Regression model---despite achieving a much lower overall accuracy of 68.22\%---leads the entire leaderboard in raw Severe/Hazardous Recall (86.58\%). The scientific reason for this behavior is that Logistic Regression constructs a soft, linear decision boundary across overlapping features. In highly imbalanced settings, this linear boundary naturally extends further, capturing outlying severe pollutant spikes at the cost of higher false positives (which decreases overall accuracy). Conversely, highly complex non-linear models like standard Random Forests or pre-augmented LSTMs overfit to the dominant majority classes, creating tight non-linear boundaries that completely ignore the rare 'Severe' instances, resulting in a disastrous 0.0\% recall on the most critical public health class. Our VAE generative oversampling successfully mitigates this, allowing the Bi-LSTM to construct balanced decision boundaries and achieve a robust 23.50\% Hazardous Recall while preserving its exceptional 88.08\% accuracy, achieving the optimal operational balance.

\section{Conclusion and Future Scope}
In conclusion, this research successfully demonstrates a highly scalable, multi-scalar Predictive Analytics framework that resolves the critical operational challenges of urban air quality forecasting. By combining unsupervised PCA-reduced K-Means clustering for pollutant regime mapping, a Deep Variational Autoencoder (VAE) for physically consistent minority class oversampling, and an attention-weighted Bidirectional LSTM network, we successfully address the extreme class imbalance that has historically caused sequential models to ignore life-threatening pollution spikes. Our empirical results confirm that while classical linear models like Logistic Regression provide a valuable, high-recall safety net due to their soft decision boundaries, the attention-weighted BiLSTM network trained on VAE-augmented data delivers the most robust performance, achieving 88.08\% accuracy and 0.822 Macro F1-score while boosting Hazardous class recall from 0.0\% to 23.50\%. Ultimately, wrapping this deep quantitative pipeline in a factually grounded, ReAct-based Agentic AI chatbot provides a vital explainable interface that bridges complex machine learning with transparent, real-world conversational decision support for public health safety.

The future scope of this research project centers on scaling the operational deployment and expanding the dataset inputs to further improve prediction accuracy and spatial granularity. First, we propose integrating multi-spectral satellite imagery indices, such as tropospheric $\text{NO}_2$ and $\text{CO}$ columns from the Sentinel-5P TROPOMI sensor, to supplement ground-level monitoring stations and provide continuous spatial coverage across remote areas. Second, we aim to transition the centralized training architecture into a Federated Learning framework, allowing individual municipal edge-devices to localise model parameters without sharing raw pollutant data, thereby preserving municipal data privacy. Finally, we plan to implement Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents within the conversational interface to simulate the economic and health impacts of proactive policy decisions, such as regional traffic restrictions or industrial shutdowns, transforming the agent from a passive forecasting assistant into an active environmental policy simulator.
